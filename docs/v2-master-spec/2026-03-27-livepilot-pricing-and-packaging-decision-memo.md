# LivePilot Pricing And Packaging Decision Memo

Date: 2026-03-27  
Status: Decision-oriented strategy memo  
Scope: Recommended product packaging, launch sequence, pricing posture, upgrade
logic, and technical implications for commercializing the LivePilot product line

---

## 1. Purpose

The broader product-line strategy document explains how LivePilot could become a
family of products.

This memo makes that direction more concrete by answering five practical
questions:

1. What should the product family actually be called?
2. Which editions should exist first, and which should wait?
3. How many SKUs should be sold at each stage?
4. What pricing posture best fits this market and this product?
5. What technical and operational work is required to make packaging credible?

This memo is intentionally more decisive than the product-line strategy. It is
meant to guide execution, not just discussion.

---

## 2. Executive Decision Summary

### Decision 1: Keep `V2` internal

`V2` should remain the internal roadmap and architecture language.

Do not replace it with:

- `LivePilot+`
- `LivePilot Pro`
- `LivePilot+ Pro`

Those are weaker external expressions than the actual product transformation now
underway.

### Decision 2: Use a product-family naming model

Recommended family:

- `LivePilot`
- `LivePilot Studio`
- `LivePilot Lab`
- reserve `LivePilot Performer` for later

### Decision 3: Do not launch all editions at once

The product family should exist strategically before it exists commercially.

Recommended rollout:

- Phase A: `LivePilot Studio` is the first serious commercial hero
- Phase B: introduce a clearly bounded base `LivePilot` edition if it helps
  acquisition and differentiation
- Phase C: launch `LivePilot Lab` only after retrieval, critique, and corpus
  workflows are real enough to justify it
- Phase D: keep `LivePilot Performer` off the public pricing page until live
  trust is proven

### Decision 4: Prefer perpetual local ownership plus updates

Recommended pricing posture:

- local functionality should not disappear when a subscription ends
- initial licenses should be perpetual or perpetual-like
- recurring revenue should come from update entitlement, support, cloud add-ons,
  or advanced institutional offerings

### Decision 5: Package value by workflow depth, not by arbitrary lockouts

Edition boundaries should mostly be expressed through workflows:

- base = control, clarity, stable memory
- Studio = listening, retrieval, critique
- Lab = corpus, research, advanced exploration
- Performer = live-safe co-agency later

This creates cleaner product logic and reduces resentment around pricing.

## 3. Recommended Packaging Model

## 3.1 Brand and SKU hierarchy

### Umbrella brand

Use `LivePilot` as the product family and umbrella identity.

### Near-term sellable products

The first sellable products should be:

- `LivePilot Studio`
- possibly `LivePilot` base later or in parallel, depending on onboarding and
  differentiation strength

### Later sellable products

- `LivePilot Lab`
- `LivePilot Performer` only after dedicated validation

## 3.2 Why not launch all editions at once

Launching too many SKUs too early creates preventable problems:

- confusing purchase decisions
- weak differentiation
- shallow marketing stories
- more entitlement and support complexity
- pressure to invent tier walls before the workflows are mature

### Practical rule

Do not launch a separate edition until the capability boundary behind it is
clear enough that users can understand it in one sentence.

By that standard:

- `Studio` is ready to become a future hero
- `Lab` is conceptually clear but not yet product-ready
- `Performer` is still research, not packaging

## 4. Recommended Commercial Rollout

## 4.1 Phase A: Hero-launch with `LivePilot Studio`

This is the most practical initial commercial move.

### Why

- the strongest differentiator is not basic control
- the strongest differentiator is hearing plus retrieval plus critique
- that is what makes the product feel new
- a flagship story is easier to market than a low-end utility story

### Recommended external framing

`LivePilot Studio` becomes the first clear public promise:

- analyze sections
- compare to references
- find related material
- receive critique inside the session

### Internal product implication

This does not mean the base product disappears.

It means:

- the shared core continues to exist
- the flagship commercial message centers on Studio

## 4.2 Phase B: Introduce base `LivePilot` deliberately

Base `LivePilot` should be introduced when at least one of the following is
true:

- acquisition needs a lower-friction entry point
- the base product has a coherent value proposition on its own
- onboarding can clearly explain the difference between base and Studio

### Base value proposition

Base `LivePilot` should not be "Studio minus everything good."

It should stand on its own as:

- stable Live control
- better navigation and context
- foundational memory
- a trustworthy automation and orchestration layer

## 4.3 Phase C: Launch `LivePilot Lab`

Lab should be a later expansion once:

- retrieval is clearly useful
- critique is trusted
- corpus workflows are deeper than demos
- advanced users are already asking for more open-ended research workflows

### How to launch Lab

Not through a mass-market splash.

Better approach:

- early access
- design partners
- educator and institution pilots
- artist-researcher case studies

## 4.4 Phase D: Keep `Performer` off the public shelf until it is safe

The performer story is exciting, but it is the easiest place to overpromise.

Practical rule:

- if it is not safe and legible on stage, it is not a product yet

## 5. Recommended Pricing Posture

## 5.1 Core recommendation

Use:

- perpetual local license
- 12 months of updates included
- paid renewal or maintenance-style update plan after that

This is the most credible fit for:

- local-first architecture
- artist trust
- plugin and creative-software expectations
- the seriousness of the workflow value

## 5.2 Why not subscription-first

Subscription-first would create avoidable problems:

- it would make LivePilot feel more like a SaaS AI app than a serious music tool
- it would create pressure to justify billing with hype instead of depth
- it would create resistance before the product has established trust
- it would weaken the local-first story

This does not mean "never use subscription."

It means:

- do not make subscription the foundation of the commercial identity

## 5.3 Where subscription can still make sense

Subscription can make sense later for:

- optional cloud inference or hosted models
- large-scale library or cloud corpus services
- institution support plans
- premium update memberships
- collaborative or hosted analytics features if they become real

Those are service layers, not the foundation of the base value proposition.

## 7. Recommended Pricing Architecture

These are the most practical starting hypotheses, not permanently fixed public
prices.

## 7.1 Recommended near-term public pricing

### Option to avoid

Avoid launching three paid editions at once.

That creates too many open questions too early:

- which one should I buy
- what am I missing
- why is this one more expensive
- which one will still matter in six months

### Recommended initial paid offer

`LivePilot Studio`

Suggested initial public pricing:

- early access / early adopter: `USD 199`
- standard target price after validation: `USD 249`
- includes 12 months of updates

Why this makes sense:

- it is above utility-level pricing, which signals seriousness
- it is still below the psychological range where buyers expect a full DAW-scale
  suite
- it aligns more with "high-value creative workflow tool" than with "cheap AI
  gimmick"

## 7.2 Recommended base edition pricing

If and when base `LivePilot` launches as a clearly distinct edition:

- target range: `USD 99` to `USD 129`
- include 12 months of updates

Why:

- it remains competitive with premium utilities
- it offers a lower-friction entry point
- it protects Studio differentiation without making base feel disposable

## 7.3 Recommended Lab pricing

Do not hard-launch public Lab pricing before the workflows are validated.

Current recommendation:

- individual early access target: `USD 399` to `USD 599`
- institution pricing: quote-based or seat-based
- likely stronger fit as annual research/support plan than as a simple perpetual
  utility license

Why:

- Lab is a specialist product
- advanced users and institutions care about depth, support, and reproducibility
- a too-low price would under-signal the seriousness of the offer

## 7.4 Upgrade pricing recommendation

If base `LivePilot` and `Studio` both exist publicly:

- `LivePilot` -> `Studio` upgrade should feel generous, not punitive
- target upgrade logic: users pay the difference or slightly less than the
  difference within a defined window

Recommended working rule:

- if a user bought base within the last 12 months, most of that spend should
  count toward Studio

That preserves trust and reduces hesitation.

## 7.5 Update plan recommendation

Suggested model:

- 12 months of updates included
- optional renewal for continued updates
- product remains usable locally without renewal

Potential renewal bands:

- base: `USD 39` to `USD 59/year`
- Studio: `USD 79` to `USD 99/year`
- Lab: case-by-case or annual support plan

These are maintenance/update numbers, not service-subscription numbers.

## 8. Recommended Trial Strategy

## 8.1 Base trial

If base `LivePilot` is a public product:

- offer a full but time-limited trial

## 8.2 Studio trial

Recommended approach:

- time-limited Studio trial or guided beta access
- ensure the user can experience:
  - section analysis
  - reference comparison
  - at least one retrieval workflow

The trial should expose the product's real value, not just the shell.

## 8.3 Lab access

Recommended approach:

- invite or application based at first
- not a mass self-serve trial

This keeps expectations cleaner and lets the team work closely with advanced
users during the early phases.

## 9. Recommended Packaging Matrix

| Dimension | LivePilot | Studio | Lab | Performer |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Core Live control | Yes | Yes | Yes | Yes |
| Session context and memory | Yes | Yes | Yes | Yes |
| Listening foundation | Limited or no | Yes | Yes | Yes later |
| Retrieval | No | Yes | Yes | Limited live-safe later |
| Critique | No | Yes | Yes | Limited live-safe later |
| Corpus workflows | No | Limited | Yes | No |
| Advanced research surfaces | No | No or limited | Yes | No |
| Live safety / cue engine | No | No | No | Yes |
| Main buyer | Advanced Live user | Producer/composer | Research user | Performer |
| Main sales motion | Self-serve | Self-serve hero | Design partner / direct | Pilot only |

### Packaging principle

Package by workflow coherence, not by nickel-and-diming primitives.

If users can understand the purpose of each edition, pricing becomes much easier
to defend.

## 10. Recommended Technical Packaging Decisions

## 10.1 One codebase

Use one entitlement-aware product core.

Do not create:

- separate repos
- heavily diverging branches
- duplicated workflow implementations per edition

## 10.2 Required internal systems

To support commercial packaging credibly, the product needs:

- edition manifests
- entitlement checks
- licensing and update-entitlement state
- edition-aware onboarding
- upgrade messaging
- compatibility and installer checks
- documentation by edition

## 10.3 Tool-surface implications

The product line should be packaged through workflows, not raw primitive counts.

That means:

- entitlement logic should focus on workflow exposure
- low-level primitives should remain stable where compatibility requires them
- internal tools should not automatically become saleable feature bullets

## 10.4 Support and release channels

Recommended channels:

- stable
- beta
- experimental

Lab and performer-oriented workflows should spend longer in beta or experimental
tracks than base or Studio control workflows.

## 11. Recommended Go-To-Market Sequence

## Stage 1: Design-partner Studio development

Audience:

- producers
- sound designers
- advanced Live users

Goal:

- validate the Studio value proposition before broad packaging complexity

Proof required:

- real sessions
- real references
- real time saved
- real decisions changed

## Stage 2: Studio commercial launch

Story:

- "LivePilot Studio gives Ableton Live better ears."

Offer:

- single flagship SKU or clearly dominant flagship SKU
- strong case-study driven launch
- trial or guided access

## Stage 3: Base edition clarification

Goal:

- define a lower-friction acquisition path without weakening Studio's story

This step should happen only when:

- the base edition has a clear standalone job
- onboarding can explain the differences cleanly

## Stage 4: Lab early access

Goal:

- convert the strongest research and sound-design users into a distinct advanced
  edition

Best motion:

- invite-only
- educator and institution partnerships
- advanced case studies

## Stage 5: Performer pilots

Goal:

- validate live trust before packaging anything for public sale

## 12. Recommended Marketing Decisions

## 12.1 The hero product should be Studio

In the near term, the marketable story is strongest when the public-facing hero
is `LivePilot Studio`.

Why:

- it speaks directly to hearing, retrieval, and critique
- it sounds like a place where serious work happens
- it avoids the generic quality of `Pro`

## 12.2 The base story should be modest and trust-building

If base `LivePilot` is marketed publicly, it should be framed as:

- control
- memory
- clarity
- workflow assistance

not as a cheaper "almost Studio."

## 12.3 The Lab story should be narrow and deep

Lab should not try to speak to everybody.

It should speak clearly to:

- sound researchers
- advanced sound designers
- experimental artists
- institutions

## 12.4 The wrong marketing move

Do not market the family as:

- "AI for every musician"
- "the all-in-one music AI platform"
- "the future of song generation"

Those categories are crowded, confusing, and not where LivePilot is strongest.

## 13. Key Risks

### Risk 1: Too many SKUs too early

Mitigation:

- make Studio the hero first

### Risk 2: Base edition feels like a crippled funnel

Mitigation:

- give base a clear, respectable purpose

### Risk 3: Lab launches before it is real

Mitigation:

- use invite-only access until corpus and research workflows are mature

### Risk 4: Pricing looks like SaaS opportunism

Mitigation:

- prefer perpetual local ownership plus updates

### Risk 5: Technical packaging lags strategy

Mitigation:

- build entitlements, onboarding, edition docs, and upgrade logic as first-class
  backlog items

- use `LivePilot`, `LivePilot Studio`, `LivePilot Lab`

- avoid `LivePilot+` and `Pro`-style naming

### Launch

- perpetual local license
- 12 months of updates included
- optional renewal after that

### Packaging

- package by workflows, not by primitive counts
- keep one codebase
- implement entitlement-aware exposure

### GTM

- design partners first

## 15. Open Questions That Should Stay Open

- whether base `LivePilot` launches simultaneously with Studio or shortly after
- whether Lab is perpetual plus maintenance or annual research membership
- whether some optional cloud/model services are sold separately later
- whether the base edition is branded simply as `LivePilot` or sometimes as
  `LivePilot Core` in internal sales materials

## 17. Final Recommendation

The strongest path is not to rename LivePilot as a generic Pro product.

The strongest path is:

- keep `V2` as the internal transformation
- let `Studio` become the flagship external expression of that transformation
- use `Lab` to capture the deeper research market later
- keep `Performer` off the public shelf until trust is earned

That gives LivePilot a credible future as:

- a serious base control system
- a flagship studio intelligence layer
- an advanced research environment
- and eventually a live co-agency platform

That is a much stronger and more defensible commercial path than a generic
premium rename.
